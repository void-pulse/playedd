# ep11 — Why Are You Tired All the Time? (LOCKED narration)

Register: neutral + wonder, reassuring landing. Features Stan. ~1,750 words / ~7 min. Minimize em dashes.

---

[COLD OPEN — withhold the answer]

Stan got eight hours last night. A full eight. In bed on time, no coffee after noon. He did everything the internet told him to.

And right now, at three in the afternoon, he is a zombie. Eyes heavy, brain static. He could put his head on the desk and be out in four seconds.

So tonight he'll go to bed early and fix it. And at eleven p.m., he is wide awake. Wired. Scrolling. Suddenly full of energy and big ideas, at exactly the wrong time.

Tired all day. Awake all night. Eight hours of sleep, and he still feels like he's running on three.

Here's the strange part. The problem usually isn't how much you slept. You could fix the sleep completely and still feel exactly like this.

Because being tired all the time isn't really about sleep at all. It's about something most of us were never told we have. And once you see it, this whole exhausting loop finally makes sense.

[HEAD-FAKE — break the obvious answer]

The obvious answer is "just sleep more." And for some people, sure. They're genuinely short on hours.

But you know people who are not. People who get their eight, nine, ten hours and still drag through the afternoon like Stan. If tiredness were only about quantity, more sleep would fix everyone. It doesn't.

So if it's not the amount, what is it?

Here's what nobody mentions. You don't have one system keeping you awake. You have two. And they do not always agree.

[REVEAL 1 — the hourglass: sleep pressure]

The first one you already feel. The longer you're awake, the more tired you get. Scientists call it sleep pressure.

All day, your brain burns fuel, and a molecule called adenosine slowly builds up. The more it piles up, the heavier your eyelids feel. Picture sand trickling into an hourglass all day. By night the glass is full. Sleep flips it over and empties it. That's why you wake up clear and fade as the hours go on.

And coffee? Caffeine doesn't remove the sand. It just tapes over the sensor that reads it. The pile is still there. You've simply stopped feeling it, until the caffeine wears off and it all crashes back at once.

So that's clock number one. Pressure that builds while you're up, and drains while you sleep.

But here's the part that changes everything. There is a second clock. And it does not care how long you've been awake.

[REVEAL 2 — the body clock and its daily timetable]

Deep in your brain, behind your eyes, sits a cluster of about twenty thousand cells. Smaller than a grain of rice. And it is the master clock for your entire body.

It runs a roughly twenty-four-hour cycle, and it decides when you feel sharp and when you feel wiped, no matter how much you slept.

And it keeps a schedule. A real one.

Late morning, it pushes you to a peak. You're at your sharpest.

Then around two to four in the afternoon, it does the opposite. It dips. Hard. That mid-afternoon wall, where your brain turns to soup? That is not your lunch. That is your clock, dimming the lights on purpose. People who skip lunch feel the exact same dip, at the exact same time.

And then at night, right when you're trying to wind down, it can do something almost cruel. It hands you a second wind. A burst of energy right before bed. That is not random. Your clock has a window in the evening where it actively fights off sleep, and if you push your bedtime into it, you will lie there wide awake. Just like Stan at eleven p.m.

So look at Stan's whole day again. The afternoon crash. The midnight energy. None of it was random. He was just reading a schedule he didn't know he had.

[REVEAL 3 — light sets the clock]

So what sets this clock? What tells those cells what time it actually is?

Light. Almost entirely light.

Inside your eyes are special cells that have nothing to do with seeing. Their only job is to measure light and report it straight to the master clock. Bright light in the morning tells the clock, "it's daytime, start the day," and locks the whole schedule into place.

But light at night does the reverse. It says, "it's still daytime, stay up," and shoves the schedule later. And blue light is the strongest signal of all. The cool, bright glow pouring out of your phone, your laptop, your TV. It suppresses melatonin, the hormone meant to make you sleepy, and nudges your clock backward.

So when Stan lies in bed scrolling to "relax," he is not relaxing. He is holding a tiny sun a few inches from his face, telling his clock the day isn't over. Night after night, pushing his own bedtime later, then blaming himself for not being tired.

[REVEAL 4 — the reframe: social jetlag]

And this is where it all comes together. The real reason so many of us feel tired all the time.

It's not that you aren't sleeping. It's that your clock and your life are set to two different time zones.

Scientists even have a name for it. Social jetlag.

Think about your week. Monday through Friday, an alarm rips you out of sleep before your body is ready. Your clock wanted more. The alarm did not care.

Then the weekend arrives. No alarm. So you sleep in. Your body finally gets to run on its own schedule, drifting later.

And then Monday slams the alarm back down, hours earlier than your body has now drifted to.

You didn't fly anywhere. But your body did. Every single weekend, you give yourself a tiny dose of jet lag. Flying west on Saturday, flying east on Monday, then spending the whole week with that groggy, wrung-out, "what time zone am I even in" feeling. Because biologically, that is exactly what you are. Jet-lagged. You just never left your house.

And this is not rare. In large studies, more than two out of three people carry at least an hour of this misalignment, every single week. Plenty are carrying two or three, the equivalent of flying across the country and back, over and over.

So if you feel tired all the time, here is the part worth hearing. You are probably not lazy. You are almost certainly not broken. You are out of sync. Your body is keeping perfect time. It is just keeping it for a different schedule than the one your life is forcing on it.

[HISTORY THREAD — segmented sleep]

And here's the strangest twist of all. Even the thing we call "normal sleep," one long unbroken block, turns out to be surprisingly new.

For most of human history, people did not sleep that way at all.

Historians digging through old diaries, court records, even lines from Homer and Virgil, kept finding the same pattern, over and over. People had a "first sleep" and a "second sleep." They would go down a couple of hours after dark, then wake up around midnight, on purpose, for an hour or two. And they used that time. They prayed, tended the fire, talked to whoever was beside them, even visited the neighbors. Then went down for their second sleep until dawn.

Waking up in the dead of night wasn't a problem to be fixed. It was just the natural seam in the middle of the night.

Then came artificial light. First lamps, then the lightbulb. We pushed our evenings later, squeezed sleep into one tight block, and quietly forgot the gap had ever been there.

Now, historians still argue about how widespread this really was, so we shouldn't oversell it. But it raises a genuinely comforting thought. If you wake at three a.m. and lie there, calm but alert, convinced something is wrong with you, maybe nothing is. Maybe you are just running an older version of the program. The one your great-great-grandparents would have recognized in a heartbeat.

[BUTTON — twist onto the viewer, land on wonder]

So, why are you tired all the time?

Usually, it's not because you are failing at sleep. It's because you've got a twenty-four-hour body trying to run on a nine-to-five world's clock. Two timers that don't agree. A master clock set by light. And a schedule built for a spinning planet, not a calendar app.

Now, one honest note. Real exhaustion that drags on for weeks and never lifts can sometimes be something medical. A thyroid running slow, low iron, sleep apnea. That's a quick visit to a doctor, not a video. But that everyday, foggy, "why am I always like this" kind of tired? That one is very often just timing.

You are not a tired person. You are a perfectly good clock, being asked to keep the wrong time.

And the next time you find yourself wide awake at midnight, buzzing with energy you can't use, you can at least smile about one thing. A few hundred years ago, that wasn't a problem at all. That was simply called being awake, between your two sleeps.

## SOURCES
- **Two-process model of sleep (Process S + Process C):** Borbély, A. "The two-process model of sleep regulation: a reappraisal" / "Beginnings and outlook," Journal of Sleep Research (2016, 2022). Process S = homeostatic sleep pressure tied to adenosine accumulation; Process C = circadian alerting signal with a mid-morning peak, ~2–4pm dip, evening wake-maintenance zone.
- **Adenosine / caffeine:** adenosine builds during wakefulness as a byproduct of brain energy use and increases sleep pressure; caffeine is an adenosine-receptor antagonist (blocks the signal, does not clear the molecule). (Borbély 2016; standard neuropharmacology.)
- **Afternoon dip is circadian, not lunch:** Process C predicts the ~2–4pm dip independent of food intake (two-process model literature; postprandial effects are secondary).
- **Suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) = master clock; light is the entraining signal:** SCN ~20,000 neurons in the anterior hypothalamus; intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (melanopsin) report light to the SCN. Morning light advances/anchors phase; evening/blue light delays phase and suppresses melatonin. (StatPearls, Neuroanatomy: Suprachiasmatic Nucleus; phase-response-curve literature; melanopsin/blue-light studies, e.g. PMC2723863, PMC2722576.)
- **Social jetlag:** Wittmann, Dinich, Merrow & Roenneberg, "Social jetlag: misalignment of biological and social time," Chronobiology International (2006); Roenneberg, "Internal Time: Chronotypes, Social Jet Lag, and Why You're So Tired." >2/3 of people carry ≥1h/week of social jetlag; evening chronotypes worst (chronotype/social-jetlag reviews, MDPI Biology 2019).
- **Segmented sleep ("first"/"second sleep"):** Ekirch, A. R., "At Day's Close: Night in Times Past"; "Segmented Sleep in Preindustrial Societies." Historical record of biphasic sleep with a midnight waking interval; references to Homer/Virgil; consolidation linked to artificial light. NOTE: extent/universality is academically debated (see "Have we lost sleep?", PMC10404514).
- **Medical caveat:** persistent fatigue (weeks+) can reflect anemia/iron deficiency, hypothyroidism, sleep apnea, or vitamin deficiency and warrants medical evaluation (Mayo Clinic Fatigue; Cleveland Clinic).
