## Description

When you were a kid, a single summer felt like it lasted forever. Now a whole year disappears in what feels like a couple of weeks. The clock never changed. So why does time keep speeding up? The answer has almost nothing to do with time, and everything to do with what your brain quietly throws away.

What you'll learn:
- Why the "obvious" answer (a year is a smaller fraction of your life) is tidy, popular, and mostly wrong
- How your brain actually measures the past: by the density of new memories, not by the clock
- Why childhood felt so long, and why adult routine makes whole years vanish in hindsight
- The debated second theory: your brain may take in fewer mental "snapshots" per second as it ages
- The one thing that measurably slows time back down, and it isn't a clock

Every claim is sourced below, including the popular version the research doesn't actually support.

What year did this start happening to you? The one that felt like it lasted a week. Drop it in the comments.

SOURCES
- Proportional theory: Paul Janet (1877), popularized via William James. Intuitive, but it does not fit how time actually feels on its own (used here as the "obvious but weak" answer).
- Memory / novelty-encoding theory (the core): we judge how long a past stretch felt by the density of distinct, novel memories in it. Childhood novelty makes dense encoding, so it reads long in hindsight; adult routine and automaticity compress. (Psychology Today; recent work on temporal landmarks.)
- Bejan, A. (2019), European Review: proposes that neural image-processing slows with age (fewer frames per second). Presented here as a debated, secondary idea, not settled science.
- Novelty slows perceived time: well supported. Novel experiences are richly encoded in the hippocampus and sensory cortex, expanding remembered duration. (University of Michigan Psychology; research summaries.)
