# Script — Movie Theaters Don't Make Money on Movies

**[COLD OPEN]**
When you buy a movie ticket, the theater barely keeps any of it. On a big opening weekend, almost all of that money goes straight back to the studio. The theater is lucky to keep a few cents on the dollar. So here's the real question. If they make almost nothing on the movie, how is the theater still open? The answer is sitting in your lap. It's the popcorn.

**[SETUP]**
Start with the ticket, because it's worse than you think. When a new blockbuster opens, the studio sets the terms, and the terms are brutal. For the first couple of weeks the studio can take most of the money from every ticket sold. The theater's slice only grows the longer the film sticks around, which is exactly why some movie you forgot existed is still quietly playing on screen nine in week six. That's not leftover scheduling. That's the theater finally getting paid.

And raising ticket prices barely helps, because about 70 cents of every extra dollar gets handed right back to the studio too. So after rent, staff, air conditioning for a giant dark room, and a projector that costs more than a house, the average theater runs on a profit margin of around four percent. A vending machine in a hospital does better than that.

**[THE TURN]**
So they went hunting for the one thing studios don't get a cut of. Snacks.

A serving of popcorn costs the theater under fifty cents to make. Kernels are dirt cheap, they puff up to twenty times their size, and a squirt of fake butter finishes the job. Then they sell it to you for nine dollars. That is a markup of nearly thirteen hundred percent. And you'll pay it, because where exactly are you going to go? You're sealed in a dark box for two hours with one snack counter and a strict no-outside-food rule. That's not hospitality. That's a monopoly the size of a lobby.

The margin on that popcorn sits around 85 percent. We don't have to guess at that. AMC's own financial filings showed more than 800 million dollars in food and drink sales against about 150 million in cost. An 81 percent margin, straight off the books of the biggest chain in the country.

Here's the line that flips the whole thing. Concessions are only about a fifth of what a theater takes in. But they're nearly half of its actual profit. The movie fills the seats. The popcorn pays the rent. As one theater owner whose family had been in the business since 1908 put it: "We left the movie business. We're now in the popcorn business."

**[THE DEEPER TURN]**
And now the part almost nobody knows. **[HUMAN LAYER]** The thing keeping theaters alive today is the exact thing they once tried to ban.

A hundred years ago, popcorn was forbidden inside movie theaters. Owners wanted to feel like grand opera houses. Plush carpets, chandeliers, a certain kind of crowd. Popcorn was loud, messy, and smelled like the street, so they banned it outright. Vendors set up carts on the sidewalk, and some theaters made you check your popcorn at the coat counter like a jacket.

Then the Great Depression hit. Money disappeared, and a movie ticket became one of the only escapes people could still afford. A bag of popcorn cost a nickel. That much, they could afford. And the theaters that swallowed their pride and started selling it survived. The ones too proud to sell it died. One of the first to really crack it was Julia Braden, a widow in Kansas City who talked her way into a theater lobby and built a popcorn empire while fancier theaters around her went broke. There was even a Dallas chain that put popcorn machines in eighty of its theaters but refused to put them in its five fanciest locations, because those were "too classy" for popcorn. Within two years, the eighty were thriving. The five holdouts were bleeding money.

Then World War Two rationed sugar, which gutted candy and soda. Popcorn needed no sugar, so it walked in and took the throne. By 1945, more than half the popcorn eaten in America was eaten inside a movie theater.

**[PAYOFF]**
So here's the real shape of the business. The movie is the bait. The popcorn is the trap. The whole building, the lobby, the buttery smell pumped into the air, the path that funnels you right past the counter, all of it is engineered to sell you the snack. The film is just the reason you walked in the door.

**[BUTTON]**
So next time you grumble about a nine dollar popcorn, here's the truth. You're not overpaying for a snack. You're paying the theater's rent. The ticket was never the point.

You got played.
