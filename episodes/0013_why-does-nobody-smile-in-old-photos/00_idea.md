# Episode Idea — Why Does Nobody Smile in Old Photos?
Date: 2026-06-10

- **Vein:** Deep Time / history (rotates off the mind cluster).
- **Working title:** Why Does Nobody Smile in Old Photos?
- **Hook (cold open):** Stan flips through a box of old family photos, every ancestor stone-faced like a row of mugshots. Not one smile. Were they all miserable?
- **The reframe (it's not X, it's Y):** It's not that life was grim or that exposures were too long. Smiling at a camera was seen as undignified and foolish, and the "say cheese" reflex is a ~100-year-old habit a camera company taught us.
- **EMOTIONAL REGISTER:** neutral + wonder, light and fun. Not doom. Reassuring twist (your ancestors weren't joyless).
- **Head-fake:** the popular answer = long exposure times (hold still, can't hold a smile). True at the very start (1840s, ~15-min exposures) but exposures got fast within decades and people STILL didn't smile into the 1900s, so that's not the real reason. Break it.
- **Verified facts (see 01_script.md SOURCES):** early daguerreotypes needed long exposures, but tech advanced fast; smiles only became standard 1920s-30s; photography inherited painted-PORTRAIT convention where a wide grin = madness/drunkenness/lower-class/foolish; studios said "say prunes"; rarity/expense made photos solemn formal occasions; Kodak (George Eastman, $1 Brownie 1900) democratized casual snapshots and shifted photos from dignified portraits to happy-moment snapshots; "say cheese" emerged ~1940s. Bad-teeth theory = largely a MYTH.
- **MYTH-RISK:** long-exposure is partly true (head-fake), don't present it as the whole reason; bad-teeth = debunk or omit; frame the convention/culture + Kodak shift as the real driver.
