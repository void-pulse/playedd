# Script — Why Is Every World Map Wrong?

(Narration only. Short paragraphs, one idea each. Spoken style. Mark the human-layer beat inline with [HUMAN LAYER]. Minimize em dashes. Spine maps to brand/STORY.md. EMOTIONAL REGISTER: neutral + wonder, light; NOT cynical/conspiracy. Stan sparingly. TARGET ~8 min / ~1,400 words at Jack's 0.92x read.)

**[COLD OPEN]**
The map of the world you have looked at your whole life, on the classroom wall, in the textbook, on your phone, is wrong. Not a little off. Wrong in a way that has quietly warped how you picture the entire planet. Here is a quick example. Look at Greenland. It looks enormous, about the size of all of Africa, right? Africa is fourteen times bigger. Fourteen. And here is the strange part. That is not a mistake somebody could fix. Every flat map of Earth is wrong, on purpose, and it has to be.

**[SETUP]**
You have one picture of the world burned into your brain. The familiar shapes, the familiar sizes. And a huge amount of what it tells you about how big things are is distorted, sometimes wildly. Stan has been looking at it his whole life and never once questioned it. Almost nobody does. So what is going on?

**[THE TURN]**
Here is the core of it, and it is not a conspiracy. It is geometry. You cannot take a sphere and flatten it onto a flat rectangle without stretching, squishing, or tearing it. Try it yourself. Peel an orange and try to press the peel flat on a table. It rips. It warps. It will not lie down cleanly, ever. The Earth is a ball. Your map is a rectangle. So something has to give.

And this is not just difficult. It is mathematically impossible, and we have known that for almost two hundred years. In 1827, the mathematician Carl Friedrich Gauss actually proved that a curved surface like a sphere can never be flattened without distortion. He gave it a grand name, the Remarkable Theorem. So this is not a problem we are too dumb to solve, or one a smarter cartographer will crack next year. It is baked into the shape of the planet. Every flat map has to lie about something. The only question is what it chooses to lie about.

**[REVEAL — Mercator's bargain]**
The map you know best is called the Mercator projection. It was made in 1569, by a man named Gerardus Mercator. And crucially, he did not make it to show you the true sizes of countries. He made it for sailors. Mercator pulled off one genuinely brilliant trick. He kept all the directions perfectly accurate. On his map, you can draw a straight line, follow one steady compass bearing, and actually get where you are going. For navigating a ship across an open ocean, that was priceless. But that trick came at a price. To keep the directions right, he had to let the sizes go wrong. And the closer you get to the top and bottom of the map, the more bloated everything becomes.

**[REVEAL — the distortions]**
So everything near the poles gets inflated, sometimes massively. Greenland balloons up to look the size of Africa, when it is fourteen times smaller. Alaska looks gigantic. Russia, Canada, and Europe all look far bigger than they really are. And meanwhile, everything near the equator gets quietly shrunk. Africa, India, South America, all squashed down. And Africa is the big one here, because Africa is genuinely colossal. It is so large you could fit the United States, China, India, and most of Europe inside it, all at once, with room to spare. But the map you grew up with makes it look almost modest.

**[HUMAN LAYER]**
And that does something subtle to your head. The countries up at the top of the map look big, dominant, important. The countries around the middle look small. A four hundred and fifty year old chart, built to sail wooden ships, has quietly shaped how billions of people picture the whole planet, which places feel large and powerful, and which feel small. You did not decide that. A sixteenth-century navigation tool decided it for you, and you inherited the picture without ever being told it was a sailor's shortcut.

And people have started to take that seriously. In 2017, the Boston public school system did something almost nobody had done before. They pulled the Mercator maps off the classroom walls and replaced them with a different projection, one that shows the true sizes, specifically because they worried that generations of kids had grown up with a quietly shrunken picture of Africa and South America. A map is never just a map. It is the first picture of the world a child ever gets.

**[REVEAL — the maps that tried to fix it]**
So people have tried to build a better one. And every attempt is fascinating, because every attempt has to pick its poison.

There is the Gall-Peters map, the one Boston chose. It nails the sizes, every country finally in correct proportion. But to do it, it stretches all the shapes into strange vertical smears, so the continents look weirdly tall and melted, like the whole world is dripping.

There is the Robinson, which National Geographic adopted in 1988 because it simply looked pleasant and balanced, gently wrong about everything but jarringly wrong about nothing. Ten years later they swapped it for yet another, the Winkel Tripel, which trades distortions even more smoothly. And in 2016, a Japanese architect won a top design award for a map called the AuthaGraph, made by slicing the globe into careful pieces and folding it almost like origami, keeping the proportions startlingly true. It looks beautiful. It also looks nothing like the world you think you know.

**[PAYOFF]**
So which map is the right one? Here is the twist. None of them. Each one is just a different choice about which lie you are willing to live with, size, or shape, or distance, or direction. The Gall-Peters tells the truth about size and lies about shape. The Mercator does the exact reverse. There is no perfect flat map of the Earth. There never can be, and now you know exactly why, because a man proved it two centuries ago. The map is not wrong by accident. It is wrong by geometry. The only truly honest map of a globe is a globe.

**[BUTTON]**
So the next time you glance at a world map and feel like you know what the planet looks like, remember what you are actually looking at. A four hundred and fifty year old tool, built to steer ships, quietly pretending to be the truth. The real world does not look like that. There is exactly one accurate map of Earth, and it is not hanging on any wall. It is the giant ball spinning silently under your feet, right now.

## SOURCES
- A sphere cannot be flattened to a plane without distortion: **Gauss's Theorema Egregium ("Remarkable Theorem"), 1827** — a surface's curvature is intrinsic, so a sphere (nonzero curvature) cannot map to a plane (zero curvature) without distortion. Every flat projection trades off area, shape, distance, or direction. (Standard differential geometry; cartography.)
- Mercator projection (Gerardus Mercator, 1569): conformal, preserves angles/compass bearings for navigation; distorts area increasingly toward the poles. (Standard cartography; Wikipedia "Mercator projection.")
- Greenland appears ~Africa-sized but Africa is ~14x larger; Africa can contain the US, China, India, and much of Europe combined. (True-size comparisons; thetruesize.com.)
- High-latitude regions (Greenland, Alaska, Russia, Canada, Europe) inflated; equatorial regions (Africa, India, South America) understated.
- **Boston Public Schools, 2017:** replaced Mercator with Gall-Peters in classrooms; first US public school district to do so (cited concern over distorted size perception of the global south). (WBUR; NPR; The World/PRX, 2017.)
- Alternatives + their tradeoffs: **Gall-Peters** (equal-area, shapes stretched/elongated); **Robinson** (compromise, adopted by National Geographic 1988); **Winkel Tripel** (compromise, replaced Robinson at NatGeo in 1998); **AuthaGraph** (Hajime Narukawa, won Japan's Good Design Grand Award 2016; near-true proportions via an origami-like subdivision). No flat map is distortion-free; a globe is the only undistorted representation. (Wikipedia "Robinson projection," "Winkel tripel projection"; Dezeen/Designboom on AuthaGraph 2016/2017.)
