# Script — An Ad Agency Invented Your Engagement Ring

**[COLD OPEN]**
That diamond engagement ring you think is some ancient romantic tradition? It's younger than sliced bread. It was invented in 1947. By an ad agency. To sell rocks nobody wanted. You didn't follow your heart. You followed a marketing campaign. Here's how they got you.

**[SETUP]**
Start with the lie underneath all of it. Diamonds aren't rare. Plenty of gemstones are far rarer. The only reason diamonds feel rare is that one company, De Beers, spent a century controlling almost the entire supply, locking most of it away and releasing a trickle to keep prices sky high. They never had a shortage. They had too many. And by the 1930s, with the Great Depression crushing demand, almost nobody was buying.

**[THE TURN]**
So in 1938 they hired an ad agency, N.W. Ayer, with an impossible job. Make people crave a rock they didn't need, at a price they couldn't justify. And Ayer cracked it with one of the most brilliant, cynical ideas in advertising history. Don't sell the diamond. Sell the love. In 1947 a copywriter named Frances Gerety wrote four words that rewired the whole planet. A diamond is forever. Your love lasts forever, so prove it with something that lasts forever. Just like that, a shiny rock stopped being jewelry and became the only acceptable proof that you actually meant it.

They put diamonds in the movies. On the movie stars. In every proposal scene in America. In 1940, about one in ten brides got a diamond ring. By 1990, it was eight in ten. They didn't tap into a tradition. They built one from scratch and made it feel a thousand years old.

**[THE PRICE]**
Then came the masterstroke. There was no rule for how much to spend, so they invented one. First they suggested one month's salary. People paid it. So they quietly bumped it to two. How can you make two months' salary last forever? Three months in some markets. Sit with that. A company you've never met decided how many weeks of your income your love is worth, and you wrote the check.

**[HUMAN LAYER]**
And here's the part that should really get you. They ran the exact same play on entire countries. Take Japan. In 1960, almost nobody there proposed with a diamond. It simply wasn't part of the culture. De Beers rolled in with the same campaign, and within twenty years, sixty percent of Japanese brides had one. A timeless tradition that didn't exist anywhere on earth until a company decided to sell it.

**[PAYOFF]**
And the final twist, the one that stings. The diamond isn't even worth what you paid. Try to sell your ring back and you'll be lucky to get half. It's not an investment. It never was. And De Beers knew. One of their own ads actually joked, "Of course there's a return on your investment. We just can't print it here." Read that again. That's them telling you to your face that there is no return. They sold you an overpriced rock, convinced you it was love, and made very sure you'd never sell it.

**[BUTTON]**
So the ancient tradition was written in 1947. The rock isn't rare. The price was made up. And the whole thing existed to empty out a warehouse. You didn't propose with a diamond because of love, or history. You did it because an ad told you to.

You got played.
