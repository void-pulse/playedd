## Description

You had all day to do one thing. You didn't. Then came the guilt. Here is the twist: the guilt is the lie, not the laziness. You are not broken and you are not weak. Your brain is a million-year-old survival machine built to do one thing brilliantly, not waste energy. And the reason resting makes you feel like a sinner? That was invented, by a monk alone in the desert around 1,600 years ago.

What you'll learn:
- Why evolution built you to do as little as possible, and why that is a feature, not a flaw
- The "law of least effort": how your brain quietly acts as an energy accountant
- How a 4th-century desert monk's "noonday demon" (acedia) became the deadly sin of sloth
- How the factory era rebranded resting from a normal human thing into a moral failure
- The one small, science-backed lever that actually helps (hint: it is not shaming yourself)

Every claim is sourced below, including the history most people get slightly wrong.

SOURCES
- Brain/body energy conservation and the "law of least effort" (the brain as a metabolic "accountant"; we unconsciously minimize movement energy). Big Think, "How evolution made our brains lazy"; Simon Fraser University walking-energetics work; Cheval & Boisgontier on effort and sedentary attraction. Note: the brain "tends to" minimize effort, not an absolute law.
- Evolutionary calorie-scarcity logic for conserving energy.
- Acedia, the "noonday demon," and Evagrius Ponticus's eight temptations (4th c.); Gregory the Great condensed the list to the seven deadly sins (~6th c.), with acedia folded into sloth; Aquinas defined sloth as "sorrow about spiritual good." Atlas Obscura, "Before Sloth Meant Laziness"; Wikipedia "Sloth (deadly sin)" / "Seven deadly sins."
- The Protestant work ethic moralizing idleness; industrial-era clocks and "living to work." Max Weber, "The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism" (a widely-cited thesis; framed here as "a new idea took hold," not single-cause history).
