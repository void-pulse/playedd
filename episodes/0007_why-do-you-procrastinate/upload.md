## Description

You have one thing to do. Instead, you clean the kitchen, answer emails, and watch "just one" video, and you feel terrible the whole time. Here is the giveaway: that is not laziness. A lazy person would be relaxed. You are suffering. Procrastination is almost the opposite of laziness, it has almost nothing to do with time, and the ancient Greeks were so obsessed with it they gave it its own name.

What you'll learn:
- Why procrastination is the opposite of laziness (and why "try harder" advice keeps failing)
- The real thing you are avoiding, and it is not the work
- Why your brain treats "future you" like a stranger who gets stuck with the bill
- The ancient Greek word for doing what you know you shouldn't, and the confession hidden inside the word "procrastinate"
- The most backwards-sounding fix in psychology, backed by a real study

Every claim is sourced below.

SOURCES
- Procrastination as short-term mood repair / emotion regulation, not time management; the cost is paid by the "future self." (Sirois, F. & Pychyl, T., 2013, "Procrastination and the Priority of Short-Term Mood Regulation," Social and Personality Psychology Compass.)
- Akrasia, the ancient Greek "weakness of will," acting against your better judgment, debated by Socrates and Aristotle. (Wikipedia "Akrasia"; Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy.)
- Etymology: Latin pro ("forward") + cras ("tomorrow") = procrastinare, "to put off until tomorrow." (Etymonline; Merriam-Webster.)
- Self-forgiveness reduces future procrastination, mediated by reduced negative affect. (Wohl, M., Pychyl, T. & Bennett, S., 2010, "I forgive myself, now I can study," Personality and Individual Differences; 119 first-year students.)
